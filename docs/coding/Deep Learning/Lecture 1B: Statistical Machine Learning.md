These notes collect the mathematical derivations from the slide deck. I use standard LaTeX notation instead of the slide-local macros:

- data point: $(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i)$
- feature matrix: $X\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times d}$
- label vector: $\mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^m$
- weights: $\mathbf{w}$ or $\theta$
- prediction: $\hat y_i=\mathbf{x}_i^\top\mathbf{w}$
- dataset: $\mathcal{D}=\{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^m$

## 1. Linear Regression

### 1.1 Model

For one example,

$$
\hat y
=\sum_{j=1}^d x_jw_j
=\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{w}.
$$

For all $m$ examples, stack the row vectors $\mathbf{x}_i^\top$ into $X$:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{y}}=X\mathbf{w}.
$$

### 1.2 Mean Squared Error

The residual on example $i$ is $\hat y_i-y_i$. The empirical mean squared error is

$$
\operatorname{MSE}(\mathbf{w})
=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m(\hat y_i-y_i)^2
=\frac{1}{m}\|X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y}\|_2^2.
$$

Learning is the optimization problem

$$
\mathbf{w}^\star
=\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}\in\mathbb{R}^d}
\frac{1}{m}\|X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y}\|_2^2.
$$

### 1.3 Ordinary Least Squares Derivation

Let

$$
\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})
=\frac{1}{m}\|X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y}\|_2^2.
$$

Expand the quadratic:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})
&=\frac{1}{m}(X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y})^\top(X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y})\\
&=\frac{1}{m}\left(
\mathbf{w}^\top X^\top X\mathbf{w}
-2\mathbf{y}^\top X\mathbf{w}
+\mathbf{y}^\top\mathbf{y}
\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Use the standard matrix derivatives

$$
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\left(\mathbf{w}^\top A\mathbf{w}\right)
=(A+A^\top)\mathbf{w},
\qquad
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\left(\mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{w}\right)=\mathbf{b}.
$$

Here $A=X^\top X$ is symmetric, so

$$
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\mathcal{L}
=\frac{1}{m}\left(2X^\top X\mathbf{w}-2X^\top\mathbf{y}\right).
$$

At an optimum of this convex quadratic, set the gradient to zero:

$$
\frac{1}{m}\left(2X^\top X\mathbf{w}-2X^\top\mathbf{y}\right)=0.
$$

Thus

$$
X^\top X\mathbf{w}=X^\top\mathbf{y}.
$$

These are the normal equations. If $X^\top X$ is invertible,

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{w}^\star=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\mathbf{y}
}
$$

and

$$
X^\dagger=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top
$$

is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse in the full-column-rank case, so

$$
\mathbf{w}^\star=X^\dagger\mathbf{y}.
$$

### 1.4 Why the Least-Squares Objective Is Convex

The Hessian is

$$
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}^2\mathcal{L}
=\frac{2}{m}X^\top X.
$$

For any vector $\mathbf{v}$,

$$
\mathbf{v}^\top X^\top X\mathbf{v}
=(X\mathbf{v})^\top(X\mathbf{v})
=\|X\mathbf{v}\|_2^2
\ge 0.
$$

Therefore $X^\top X\succeq 0$, so $\mathcal{L}$ is convex. If $X^\top X\succ 0$, the minimizer is unique. If $X^\top X$ has zero eigenvalues, there is a flat valley of minimizers.

### 1.5 Ridge Regression

Add an $\ell_2$ penalty:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ridge}}(\mathbf{w})
=\|X\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{y}\|_2^2+\lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2.
$$

Then

$$
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{ridge}}
=2X^\top X\mathbf{w}-2X^\top\mathbf{y}+2\lambda\mathbf{w}.
$$

Set this to zero:

$$
(X^\top X+\lambda I)\mathbf{w}=X^\top\mathbf{y}.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{w}_{\text{ridge}}
=(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top\mathbf{y}
}
$$

provided $\lambda>0$. The $\lambda I$ term shifts every eigenvalue upward, removing singular directions.

## 2. Gaussian Maximum Likelihood Gives MSE

### 2.1 Statistical Assumption

Assume the label is generated by a linear signal plus Gaussian noise:

$$
y=\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}+\epsilon,
\qquad
\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2).
$$

Then

$$
p(y\mid\mathbf{x};\mathbf{w})
=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x},\sigma^2)
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}
\exp\left(
-\frac{(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{w}-y)^2}{2\sigma^2}
\right).
$$

### 2.2 Log-Likelihood

For iid data,

$$
\log p(\mathcal{D}\mid\mathbf{w})
=\sum_{i=1}^m\log p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\mathbf{w}).
$$

Substitute the Gaussian density:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\log p(\mathcal{D}\mid\mathbf{w})
&=\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
-\log(\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma)
-\frac{(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\mathbf{w}-y_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}
\right]\\
&=-m\log(\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma)
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\sum_{i=1}^m(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\mathbf{w}-y_i)^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

The first term does not depend on $\mathbf{w}$, and $\sigma^2>0$ is constant, so

$$
\arg\max_{\mathbf{w}}\log p(\mathcal{D}\mid\mathbf{w})
=\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}}
\sum_{i=1}^m(\mathbf{x}_i^\top\mathbf{w}-y_i)^2.
$$

Thus Gaussian maximum likelihood is exactly least squares:

$$
\boxed{
\text{maximize Gaussian likelihood}
\iff
\text{minimize MSE}
}
$$

## 3. KL Divergence

### 3.1 Definition

For distributions $p$ and $q$,

$$
\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)
=\int p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\,dx
=\mathbb{E}_{p}\left[\log p(x)-\log q(x)\right].
$$

KL is a divergence, not a metric: generally,

$$
\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)\ne \operatorname{KL}(q\|p).
$$

### 3.2 Positive Definiteness via Jensen's Inequality

Jensen's inequality for concave $f$ says

$$
\mathbb{E}[f(X)]\le f(\mathbb{E}[X]).
$$

Since $\log$ is concave,

$$
\begin{aligned}
-\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)
&=\mathbb{E}_{p}\left[\log\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right]\\
&\le
\log\mathbb{E}_{p}\left[\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right]\\
&=\log\int p(x)\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\,dx\\
&=\log\int q(x)\,dx\\
&=\log 1\\
&=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)\ge 0.}
$$

Equality in Jensen requires $\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}$ to be constant almost surely. Since both distributions integrate to $1$, that constant must be $1$, so

$$
\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)=0
\iff
p=q
\quad\text{almost everywhere.}
$$

## 4. MLE as KL Projection

### 4.1 Empirical Distribution

The empirical distribution puts mass $1/m$ on each observed data point:

$$
p_{\text{emp}}
=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m\delta_{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i)}.
$$

The model family is

$$
\mathcal{P}_{\text{model}}
=\{p(\mathbf{x},y;\mathbf{w}):\mathbf{w}\in\Omega\}.
$$

From the variational view, learning chooses the model distribution closest to $p_{\text{emp}}$ in forward KL:

$$
\arg\min_{p\in\mathcal{P}_{\text{model}}}
\operatorname{KL}(p_{\text{emp}}\|p).
$$

### 4.2 Equivalence to Maximum Likelihood

For conditional models, use

$$
\operatorname{KL}(p(y\mid x)\|q(y\mid x))
=
\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}
\left[
\log\frac{p(y\mid x)}{q(y\mid x)}
\right].
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}\in\Omega}
\operatorname{KL}
\left(
p_{\text{emp}}\|p(\mathbf{x},y;\mathbf{w})
\right)\\
&=
\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}\in\Omega}
\int
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\delta_{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i)}(\mathbf{x},y)
\log
\frac{p_{\text{emp}}(y\mid\mathbf{x})}
{p(y\mid\mathbf{x};\mathbf{w})}
\,d\mathbf{x}\,dy\\
&=
\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}\in\Omega}
\left[
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\log p_{\text{emp}}(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i)
-
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\log p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\mathbf{w})
\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

The empirical term is constant with respect to $\mathbf{w}$, so

$$
\boxed{
\arg\min_{\mathbf{w}}
\operatorname{KL}(p_{\text{emp}}\|p_{\mathbf{w}})
=
\arg\max_{\mathbf{w}}
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\log p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\mathbf{w})
}
$$

Minimizing KL from the empirical distribution to the model is the same optimization problem as maximum likelihood.

## 5. Logistic Regression

### 5.1 Bernoulli Model

For binary classification,

$$
y\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Let

$$
p=p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x}).
$$

The Bernoulli likelihood can be written compactly as

$$
p(y\mid\mathbf{x})
=p^y(1-p)^{1-y}.
$$

Check the two cases:

$$
y=1\Rightarrow p(y\mid\mathbf{x})=p,
\qquad
y=0\Rightarrow p(y\mid\mathbf{x})=1-p.
$$

### 5.2 Logit and Sigmoid

Use a linear score, or logit,

$$
z=\theta^\top\mathbf{x}+b.
$$

Convert this real-valued score into a probability with the sigmoid:

$$
\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},
\qquad
p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x};\theta)=\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}+b).
$$

Often the bias is absorbed by appending a $1$ to $\mathbf{x}$, giving simply

$$
p_i=\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i).
$$

### 5.3 Likelihood to Binary Cross-Entropy

For one example,

$$
p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\theta)
=p_i^{y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}.
$$

Taking logs,

$$
\log p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\theta)
=
y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i).
$$

Average over the dataset:

$$
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\log p(y_i\mid\mathbf{x}_i;\theta)
=
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i\log\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i)
+(1-y_i)\log(1-\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i))
\right].
$$

Maximum likelihood maximizes this quantity. Equivalently, minimize the negative log-likelihood:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal{L}(\theta)
=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i\log\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i)
+(1-y_i)\log(1-\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i))
\right]
}
$$

This is binary cross-entropy.

### 5.4 Decision Rule

Because $\sigma$ is monotone and $\sigma(0)=0.5$,

$$
\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x})>0.5
\iff
\theta^\top\mathbf{x}>0.
$$

Thus the default classifier is

$$
\hat y=
\begin{cases}
1, & \theta^\top\mathbf{x}>0,\\
0, & \theta^\top\mathbf{x}<0.
\end{cases}
$$

The decision boundary is the hyperplane

$$
\theta^\top\mathbf{x}=0.
$$

Using a different probability threshold $c$ changes the logit threshold to

$$
\sigma(z)>c
\iff
z>\log\frac{c}{1-c}.
$$

So changing the classification threshold shifts the boundary.

## 6. Why the Sigmoid Appears

### 6.1 Generative Assumption

Assume balanced classes:

$$
p(y=1)=p(y=0)=\frac{1}{2}.
$$

Assume the class-conditional feature distribution is Gaussian with shared spherical covariance:

$$
\mathbf{x}\mid y=k\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\sigma^2I),
\qquad
k\in\{0,1\}.
$$

By Bayes' rule,

$$
p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x})
=
\frac{p(\mathbf{x}\mid y=1)p(y=1)}
{p(\mathbf{x}\mid y=1)p(y=1)+p(\mathbf{x}\mid y=0)p(y=0)}.
$$

With equal priors, the factors $1/2$ cancel:

$$
p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x})
=
\frac{
\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\boldsymbol{\mu}_1,\sigma^2I)
}{
\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\boldsymbol{\mu}_1,\sigma^2I)
+
\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\boldsymbol{\mu}_0,\sigma^2I)
}.
$$

The Gaussian normalizing constants also cancel:

$$
p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x})
=
\frac{
\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2\right)
}{
\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2\right)
+
\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2\right)
}.
$$

Divide numerator and denominator by the numerator:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p(y=1\mid\mathbf{x})
&=
\frac{1}
{1+
\exp\left(
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
+\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2
\right)
}\\
&=
\frac{1}
{1+
\exp\left(
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\left[
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
-
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2
\right]
\right)
}\\
&=
\sigma\left(
\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\left[
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
-
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2
\right]
\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

So a simple Gaussian class model leads to a sigmoid posterior probability.

### 6.2 Linear Form of the Logit

Expand the squared-distance difference:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
-
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2\\
&=
\left(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}
-2\boldsymbol{\mu}_0^\top\mathbf{x}
+\boldsymbol{\mu}_0^\top\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\right)
-
\left(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}
-2\boldsymbol{\mu}_1^\top\mathbf{x}
+\boldsymbol{\mu}_1^\top\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\right)\\
&=
2(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0)^\top\mathbf{x}
+\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
-\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore the logit is linear in $\mathbf{x}$:

$$
\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\left(
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
-
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2
\right)
=
\theta^\top\mathbf{x}+b,
$$

where

$$
\theta=\frac{\boldsymbol{\mu}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0}{\sigma^2},
\qquad
b=\frac{\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2-\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2}.
$$

Thus the sigmoid in logistic regression can be viewed as the posterior induced by two equal-covariance Gaussian class clouds.

The decision rule is also nearest-center classification:

$$
\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}+b)>0.5
\iff
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_0\|_2^2
>
\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1\|_2^2.
$$

The model predicts class $1$ exactly when $\mathbf{x}$ is closer to $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1$ than to $\boldsymbol{\mu}_0$.

## 7. Binary Classification Metrics

From the confusion matrix:

| | True $y=1$ | True $y=0$ |
|---|---:|---:|
| Predicted $\hat y=1$ | $TP$ | $FP$ |
| Predicted $\hat y=0$ | $FN$ | $TN$ |

The common hard-label metrics are

$$
\operatorname{Accuracy}
=\frac{TP+TN}{TP+FP+FN+TN},
$$

$$
\operatorname{Precision}
=\frac{TP}{TP+FP},
$$

$$
\operatorname{Recall}
=\frac{TP}{TP+FN},
$$

$$
F_1
=\frac{2PR}{P+R}.
$$

For ROC curves, use class-normalized rates:

$$
TPR=\frac{TP}{TP+FN},
\qquad
FPR=\frac{FP}{FP+TN}.
$$

If the prevalence is $1\%$ and a random scoring rule flags an $x$ fraction of every class, then

$$
TPR=\frac{0.01x}{0.01}=x,
\qquad
FPR=\frac{0.99x}{0.99}=x.
$$

The prevalence cancels, so a random score lies on the diagonal $TPR=FPR$ and has

$$
AUC=0.5.
$$

## 8. Gradient Descent

For a differentiable loss $\mathcal{L}(w)$, the gradient points uphill. Gradient descent updates in the negative-gradient direction:

$$
w\leftarrow w-\eta\nabla\mathcal{L}(w),
$$

or coordinatewise,

$$
w_j\leftarrow w_j-\eta\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial w_j}.
$$

Here $\eta>0$ is the learning rate.

## 9. Logistic Regression Gradient

### 9.1 Sigmoid Derivative

The sigmoid is

$$
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}.
$$

Differentiate:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma'(x)
&=\frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2}\\
&=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}
\left(
1-\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}
\right)\\
&=\sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)).
\end{aligned}
$$

### 9.2 Gradient of Binary Cross-Entropy

Let

$$
z_i=\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i,
\qquad
\sigma_i=\sigma(z_i).
$$

The loss is

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta)
=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i\log\sigma_i
+(1-y_i)\log(1-\sigma_i)
\right].
$$

For coordinate $\theta_j$,

$$
\frac{\partial z_i}{\partial\theta_j}=x_{ij},
\qquad
\frac{\partial\sigma_i}{\partial\theta_j}
=\sigma_i(1-\sigma_i)x_{ij}.
$$

Differentiate the loss:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_j}
&=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i\frac{1}{\sigma_i}
\frac{\partial\sigma_i}{\partial\theta_j}
+
(1-y_i)\frac{1}{1-\sigma_i}
\frac{\partial(1-\sigma_i)}{\partial\theta_j}
\right]\\
&=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i\frac{\sigma_i(1-\sigma_i)}{\sigma_i}x_{ij}
-
(1-y_i)\frac{\sigma_i(1-\sigma_i)}{1-\sigma_i}x_{ij}
\right]\\
&=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i(1-\sigma_i)
-
(1-y_i)\sigma_i
\right]x_{ij}\\
&=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\left[
y_i-y_i\sigma_i-\sigma_i+y_i\sigma_i
\right]x_{ij}\\
&=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
(y_i-\sigma_i)x_{ij}\\
&=
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
(\sigma_i-y_i)x_{ij}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the vector gradient is

$$
\boxed{
\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}
=
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
(\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i)-y_i)\mathbf{x}_i
}
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}
=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
(y_i-\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x}_i))\mathbf{x}_i.
$$

Gradient descent gives

$$
\theta
\leftarrow
\theta-\eta\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}
=
\theta
-\eta
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
(\sigma_i-y_i)\mathbf{x}_i.
$$

For a single SGD example, this becomes

$$
\theta\leftarrow\theta+\eta(y_i-\sigma_i)\mathbf{x}_i.
$$

If $y_i=1$ but $\sigma_i$ is too small, then $y_i-\sigma_i>0$, so the observed features push their weights upward.

### 9.3 One-Step SGD Example

Let one positive example have

$$
y=1,
\qquad
\sigma(\theta^\top\mathbf{x})=0.2.
$$

The residual in the update is

$$
r=y-\sigma=1-0.2=0.8.
$$

With learning rate $\eta=0.1$,

$$
\theta_j\leftarrow\theta_j+\eta r x_j
=\theta_j+0.08x_j.
$$

So every feature present in the example receives an upward weight change proportional to its count. The logit change on the same example is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta z
&=(\theta_{\text{new}}-\theta)^\top\mathbf{x}\\
&=\eta r\,\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}\\
&=\eta r\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

For example, if $\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2=25$,

$$
\Delta z=0.1\cdot0.8\cdot25=2.0.
$$

The original probability $0.2$ corresponds to

$$
z=\log\frac{0.2}{0.8}\approx -1.386.
$$

After the update,

$$
z_{\text{new}}\approx -1.386+2.0=0.614,
\qquad
\sigma(z_{\text{new}})\approx0.65.
$$

Thus the same positive example is now scored much more confidently as positive.

## 10. Why SGD Is Used

Computing a full gradient costs roughly one multiply-add per parameter per example:

$$
\text{full gradient cost}\approx md.
$$

For

$$
d=10^5,
\qquad
m=10^6,
$$

this is

$$
md=10^{11}\ \text{flops}.
$$

At $10^9$ flops/sec,

$$
\frac{10^{11}}{10^9}=100\ \text{sec}
$$

for one full-batch step.

SGD uses a minibatch $\mathcal{B}$ of size $B$:

$$
\widehat{\nabla\mathcal{L}}(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}\nabla L_i(\theta).
$$

This estimates the full gradient

$$
\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m\nabla L_i(\theta)
\approx
\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}\nabla L_i(\theta).
$$

If the minibatch is sampled uniformly, the estimator is unbiased:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{B}}
\left[
\widehat{\nabla\mathcal{L}}(\theta)
\right]
=
\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta).
$$

The SGD update is

$$
\theta\leftarrow
\theta-\eta\widehat{\nabla\mathcal{L}}(\theta).
$$

With $B=100$,

$$
Bd=100\cdot 10^5=10^7\ \text{flops},
$$

so one minibatch step is

$$
\frac{md}{Bd}=\frac{m}{B}=10^4
$$

times cheaper than one full-batch step.

An epoch still touches all data:

$$
\frac{m}{B}=10^4
$$

updates per epoch. For 10 epochs, the total cost is

$$
10\cdot md=10^{12}\ \text{flops}.
$$

At $10^9$ flops/sec,

$$
\frac{10^{12}}{10^9}=10^3\ \text{sec}\approx 17\ \text{min}.
$$

## 11. Softmax Regression

### 11.1 Categorical Model and Softmax

For $K$ classes,

$$
p(y=k\mid\mathbf{x})=\pi_k,
\qquad
\sum_{k=1}^K\pi_k=1,
\qquad
\pi_k\ge 0.
$$

A linear model produces one logit per class:

$$
\mathbf{z}=W\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^K.
$$

Softmax maps logits to probabilities:

$$
\boxed{
\pi_k
=
\frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{j=1}^K e^{z_j}}
}
$$

### 11.2 MLE to Cross-Entropy

Represent the label as a one-hot vector

$$
\mathbf{y}\in\{0,1\}^K,
\qquad
y_k=\mathbf{1}[\text{true class}=k].
$$

The categorical likelihood is

$$
p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})
=\prod_{k=1}^K\pi_k^{y_k}.
$$

The log-likelihood is

$$
\log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})
=\sum_{k=1}^K y_k\log\pi_k.
$$

The negative log-likelihood is

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal{L}
=-\sum_{k=1}^K y_k\log\pi_k
}
$$

This is the cross-entropy of the true one-hot distribution against the predicted distribution. Since $\mathbf{y}$ is one-hot, only the true class contributes:

$$
\mathcal{L}
=-\log\pi_y.
$$

Over a dataset,

$$
\mathcal{L}(W)
=
-\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m
\sum_{k=1}^K y_{ik}\log\pi_{ik}.
$$

Binary cross-entropy is the $K=2$ special case.

### 11.3 Log-Softmax Jacobian

Start from

$$
\log\pi_j
=
\log
\frac{e^{z_j}}{\sum_{\ell=1}^K e^{z_\ell}}
=
z_j-\log\sum_{\ell=1}^K e^{z_\ell}.
$$

Differentiate with respect to $z_k$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial\log\pi_j}{\partial z_k}
&=
\frac{\partial z_j}{\partial z_k}
-
\frac{1}{\sum_{\ell}e^{z_\ell}}
\frac{\partial}{\partial z_k}
\sum_{\ell}e^{z_\ell}\\
&=
\delta_{jk}
-
\frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{\ell}e^{z_\ell}}\\
&=
\delta_{jk}-\pi_k.
\end{aligned}
$$

So

$$
\boxed{
\frac{\partial\log\pi_j}{\partial z_k}
=
\delta_{jk}-\pi_k
}
$$

### 11.4 Softmax Cross-Entropy Gradient

For one example,

$$
\mathcal{L}
=-\sum_{j=1}^K y_j\log\pi_j.
$$

Differentiate with respect to logit $z_k$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial z_k}
&=
-\sum_{j=1}^K y_j
\frac{\partial\log\pi_j}{\partial z_k}\\
&=
-\sum_{j=1}^K y_j(\delta_{jk}-\pi_k)\\
&=
-\sum_{j=1}^K y_j\delta_{jk}
+
\sum_{j=1}^K y_j\pi_k\\
&=
-y_k
+
\pi_k\sum_{j=1}^K y_j.
\end{aligned}
$$

Because $\mathbf{y}$ is one-hot,

$$
\sum_{j=1}^K y_j=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial z_k}
=\pi_k-y_k
}
$$

Vector form:

$$
\boxed{
\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\mathcal{L}
=
\boldsymbol{\pi}-\mathbf{y}
}
$$

Since

$$
\mathbf{z}=W\mathbf{x},
$$

the derivative with respect to $W$ is the outer product

$$
\boxed{
\nabla_W\mathcal{L}
=
(\boldsymbol{\pi}-\mathbf{y})\mathbf{x}^\top
}
$$

For a single SGD example,

$$
\boxed{
W\leftarrow
W-\eta(\boldsymbol{\pi}-\mathbf{y})\mathbf{x}^\top
}
$$

This has the same shape as logistic regression: prediction minus truth, multiplied by the input.

## 12. Multiclass Metrics

For class $k$, compute one-vs-rest counts:

$$
P_k=\frac{TP_k}{TP_k+FP_k},
\qquad
R_k=\frac{TP_k}{TP_k+FN_k},
\qquad
F1_k=\frac{2P_kR_k}{P_k+R_k}.
$$

Overall accuracy is

$$
\operatorname{Acc}
=\frac{\#\text{correct}}{N}
=\frac{\operatorname{trace}(\text{confusion matrix})}{N},
\qquad
\operatorname{error}=1-\operatorname{Acc}.
$$

Top-$n$ accuracy is

$$
\operatorname{Top}\text{-}n\operatorname{Acc}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N
\mathbf{1}
\left[
y_i\in\operatorname{Top}_n(\mathbf{z}_i)
\right],
$$

where $\operatorname{Top}_n(\mathbf{z}_i)$ is the set of $n$ highest-scoring classes for example $i$. The corresponding error is

$$
\operatorname{Top}\text{-}n\operatorname{error}
=
1-\operatorname{Top}\text{-}n\operatorname{Acc}.
$$

Macro-F1 averages per-class F1 values equally:

$$
\operatorname{MacroF1}
=
\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K F1_k.
$$

Micro-F1 first pools $TP,FP,FN$ across classes, then computes one F1 score. For single-label multiclass classification, micro-F1 equals accuracy.

Example confusion matrix with $N=100$:

| | True A | True B | True C |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Pred A | 88 | 4 | 4 |
| Pred B | 1 | 1 | 0 |
| Pred C | 1 | 0 | 1 |

The diagonal has $88+1+1=90$ correct predictions, so

$$
\operatorname{Acc}=\frac{90}{100}=0.90.
$$

Given per-class F1 scores

$$
F1_A=0.95,
\qquad
F1_B=0.29,
\qquad
F1_C=0.29,
$$

the macro average is

$$
\operatorname{MacroF1}
=
\frac{0.95+0.29+0.29}{3}
=0.51.
$$

The micro score is dominated by the frequent class:

$$
\operatorname{MicroF1}
=
\frac{90}{90+10}
=0.90
=\operatorname{Acc}.
$$

Macro-F1 exposes the poor rare-class performance; micro-F1 largely tracks the majority-class success.

## 13. Probability Forms Used Later

The lecture closes by pointing out that many deep-learning tasks can be described as distributions:

$$
p(\mathbf{x})
\quad
\text{for generation, density modeling, and anomaly detection;}
$$

$$
p(y\mid\mathbf{x})
\quad
\text{for prediction, recognition, segmentation, and classification;}
$$

$$
p(x_t\mid x_{<t})
\quad
\text{for autoregressive sequence modeling;}
$$

$$
p(a\mid o)
\quad
\text{for control and agents.}
$$

Autoregressive modeling is a factorization of a joint distribution by the chain rule:

$$
p(x_1,\ldots,x_T)
=
\prod_{t=1}^T p(x_t\mid x_{<t}).
$$

Control can be read as conditional prediction where the output label is an action:

$$
y=a,
\qquad
\mathbf{x}=o,
\qquad
p(a\mid o)=p(y\mid\mathbf{x}).
$$
