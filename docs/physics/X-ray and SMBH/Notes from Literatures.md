## Intermediate-Mass Black Holes: the Big Picture

We describe Intermediate Black Holes as bhs with mass from $100 \sim 10^{5} M_{\odot}$.

>  #### accretion signature
>
>  An **accretion signature** means an **observable clue that a black hole is actively swallowing gas**.

>  #### ULX and HLX
>
>  Ultra-luminous X-ray sources, or ULXs, are extremely bright X-ray point sources in other galaxies, but not located at the galaxy center. Very extreme ULXs above $10^{41}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$ are often called **hyper-luminous X-ray sources**, or HLXs.  

How could we form non-stellar-mass bhs?

1.  gravitational runaway: $10^{3} - 10^{4} M_{\odot}$ seeds
2.  collapse of Population III stars: $100 M_{\odot}$ seeds
3.  “direct collapse” into a bh: $10^{4} - 10^{6} M_{\odot}$ seeds

<img src="./Notes%20from%20Literatures.assets/image-20260702210223165.png" alt="image-20260702210223165" style="zoom:50%;" />

### Seeding Model

Pop III stars will likely leave bhs around $M_{\text{BH}} \sim 100 M_{\odot}$, excepting first stars in the mass range of $1 4 0-2 6 0 ~ M_{\odot}$ that would explode as pair-instability supernovae and leave no remnant. This model is challenged by bh growing.

Direct collapsing will leave bhs around $M_{BH} \sim 10^{4} - 10^{6} M_{\odot}$, but this channel can only operate at very high redshift. The number density of halos that suffer direct collapse is very uncertain.

Gravitational runaway will produce $M_{BH} \sim 10^{3} - 10^{4} M_{\odot}$ within a dense stellar cluster.

Theory predicts many possible IMBH populations, including nuclear and wandering black holes, but uncertainties in accretion, mergers, recoil, and occupation fraction make it difficult to use present-day demographics alone to identify the true seeding channel.

### Stellar and Gas Dynamical Searches for IMBHs

If a black hole sits in a cluster or galaxy nucleus, nearby stars/gas should move faster or in a special pattern because of the black hole’s gravitational pull. If we can measure those motions precisely enough, we can infer the black-hole mass.

Mass–anisotropy degeneracy is the problem that “extra central mass” and “special stellar orbit directions” can produce similar observed stellar motions, making dynamical black-hole detections uncertain. Proper motions help because they turn a one-dimensional velocity measurement into a much more three-dimensional dynamical measurement, letting us separate “extra gravity” from “special orbit directions.”

Current dynamical measurements or limits on black hole mass in low-mass stellar systems.

Seen from the image that dynamical evidence is very ambiguous at $M_{BH} \leq 10^{4}M_{\odot}$, highlighting the importance for HLXs.

### Searches for Accreting IMBHs in Galaxy Nuclei

#### Optical Spectroscopic Selection

If an IMBH is sitting in a galaxy nucleus and accreting gas, it may behave like a scaled-down AGN. The accretion disk produces energetic radiation. That radiation ionizes surrounding gas. The gas then emits optical lines such as Hα, Hβ, [O III], [N II], [S II], etc. So astronomers look for AGN-like optical emission-line signatures in low-mass galaxies.

The method
$$
M_{\rm BH}=f_{\rm vir}\frac{r_{\rm BLR}\Delta V^2}{G}
$$

>  #### BPT Diagram
>
>  A **BPT diagram** is an optical emission-line diagnostic plot used to decide what is ionizing the gas in a galaxy: **young stars** or an **active black hole**.

Problem for BPT search: the AGNs become hopelessly intermingled with star-forming galaxies at low metallicity. 

**BPT selection**: uses optical line ratios like [O III]/Hβ and [N II]/Hα.

**He II λ4686 diagnostic**: uses the He II line, which requires high-energy photons, so people hoped it would reveal hard AGN radiation.

**Mid-infrared color cuts**: AGN often heat dust to high temperatures, giving red mid-IR colors, so this is used to find luminous AGNs.

The three methods merely give out overlaps. In low-mass, low-metallicity galaxies, many “classic AGN signs” stop being clean. Mid-IR red colors can be produced by young starbursts, not just AGN-heated dust. He II λ4686 can be powered by high-mass X-ray binaries. [O IV] can be excited by massive O stars and Wolf-Rayet stars. Even [Ne V], usually considered a strong AGN indicator because it needs very high ionization energy, can be produced by fast shocks from stellar winds or supernovae.

#### X-ray Observations Matters

High occupation is found in $M_{*} \sim 10^{9} - 10^{10} M_{\odot}$ galaxies, using the X-ray data.

>  #### Fundamental Plane
>
>  The **fundamental plane** here means the **fundamental plane of black-hole activity** or **X-ray–radio fundamental plane**. It is an empirical relation connecting three quantities:
>  $$
>  L_R,\quad L_X,\quad M_{\rm BH}.
>  $$
>  Here $L_R$ is the compact radio luminosity, usually from a jet; $L_X$ is the X-ray luminosity, usually from the inner accretion flow/corona; and $M_{\rm BH}$ is the black-hole mass.
>
>  It is called a plane because if you plot black holes in 3D space with axes
>  $$
>  \log L_R,\quad \log L_X,\quad \log M_{\rm BH},
>  $$
>  many accreting black holes lie roughly on a plane, not randomly scattered. One common form is
>  $$
>  \log L_R = \xi_X \log L_X + \xi_M \log M_{\rm BH} + b.
>  $$
>  The exact coefficients depend on calibration, but the conceptual message is: radio luminosity, X-ray luminosity, and black-hole mass are correlated in low-accretion-rate black holes with compact jets.

### Accreting Black Holes Outside of Galaxy Nuclei

#### ULX Sources

Usually ULX samples are constructed from objects for which the Eddington limit is exceeded for a typical stellar mass black hole of $\sim 10 M_{\odot}$. ULXs were initially interpreted as strong candidates for IMBHs, largely from the simple argument that inferred isotropic luminosities $\gtrsim 10^{40} \rm erg \, s^{-1}$ imply accretors  above $\sim 100 M_{\odot}$.

>  #### HLX-1
>
>  Modeling of the optical and X-ray emission through the state changes are consistent with a black hole mass of a few $10^{4} M_{\odot}$. Radio emission associated with the state changes gives similar mass estimates, in the range $M_{BH} \sim 10^{4} - 10^{5} M_{\odot}$, while fundamental plane gives out a result as high as $\sim 3 \times 10^{6} M_{\odot}$.
>
>  An intriguing puzzle in HLX-1 is the nature of the X-ray luminosity variations and state changes. Since X-ray monitoring of the source began in 2008, the state changes appeared nearly periodic at intervals of  1 yr, leading to the idea that they were associated with the orbital period of a tidally captured companion star on an eccentric orbit.

Besides HLX-1, no other very luminous ULXs are as convincing as IMBH candidates.

#### Fundamental Plane Searches in Globular Clusters

A globular cluster contains old evolved stars. Those stars lose gas through stellar winds. If an IMBH sits near the cluster center, it may capture a fraction of this gas inside its sphere of influence. The accretion flow and/or jet could then produce faint X-ray and radio emission.

### IMBH Searches with Transient Phenomena

#### TDE

Tidal disruption events (TDEs) are the electromagntic signature that may result if a star passes within its tidal radius of a black hole.

TDEs contribute to IMBH searches by temporarily lighting up otherwise invisible black holes when they disrupt stars. Their light curves and host environments can estimate or constrain black-hole masses, occupation fractions, and wandering/off-nuclear IMBH populations.

#### Gravitational Waves

Hope LISA will work.

Current limits on the merger of $\sim\, 1 0 0 \, \, M_{\odot}$ black holes are not yet constraining (Abbate et al. 2018), but should become so as LIGO progresses. The one caveat relates to the possible subset of runaway processes involving stars (as opposed to black holes) that may require low metallicity. If so, this channel also may operate only at high redshift.

## Ultraluminous X-Ray Sources: General Review for ULXs

ULXs are defined as point-like sources within or near the optical extent of a host galaxy, but away from the nucleus in order to exclude accreting supermassive black holes. The majority of ULXs are thought to be X-ray binaries powered by accretion onto a compact object and their luminosities are comparable to or above the Eddington luminosity of stellar black holes. Thus, ULXs offer a means to study either accretion near and above the Eddington limit, if the compact objects are stellar mass black holes, or unusually massive black holes.

The key physical properties of an ultraluminous X-ray binary are the mass, spin, and nature (black hole or neutron star) of the compact object, the nature of the companion star, and the orbital separation and eccentricity.

>  #### Threshold Luminosity for ULX
>
>   A comon defintion in use today is $1 \times1 0^{3 9} \, \mathrm{e r g \, s^{-1}}$ which is convenient in cgs units. Another commonly used definition is $3 \times1 0^{3 9} \, \mathrm{e r g \, s^{-1}}$ which closely corresponds to the Eddington luminosity for a $20 M_{\odot}$ black hole

ULXs are preferentially associated with star-forming galaxies, as can be inferred from the XLFs, and also with the star forming regions within galaxies.

### Comparison with Galactic X-ray Binaries

Galactic black-hole X-ray binaries (GBHBs) are the natural comparison sample, because their spectral states are much better studied. They usually move between hard, thermal, steep power-law / very-high, and intermediate states during outbursts.

The important diagnostic is not only the spectral shape, but the combination of spectrum and variability. In the thermal state, the disk should have a roughly constant inner radius, so
$$
L_{\rm disk} \propto T_{\rm in}^4.
$$
This relation is useful because the inner radius is tied to the ISCO, and therefore to the compact-object mass and spin.

But the paper stresses a warning: ULXs and Galactic binaries are often observed in different energy bands. Galactic binaries were historically observed well above a few keV, while ULXs are often observed in the softer $0.3-10\ \rm keV$ band. Therefore a direct visual comparison of spectral states can be misleading.

### X-ray Spectra

#### Simple Models

With ASCA, astronomers saw that many ULX spectra were curved, or “convex” rather than straight power laws. A curved X-ray spectrum can often be fit by an accretion disk model. In a standard disk model, the inner disk is hottest, and the spectrum has a thermal shape. The fitted inner disk temperatures were around $k T_{\text{in}} \approx 1.1–1.8 \,\rm k eV$, which is quite hot.

Some looked more like power laws, meaning the flux decreases with energy in a relatively simple way. A power-law spectrum is often associated with the hard state in Galactic black-hole binaries, where emission is dominated by Comptonized photons rather than a thermal disk.

Modern lesson: A ULX spectrum that can be fit by a simple disk or power law does not mean the source physically is just a standard disk or a hard-state black hole. It may only mean the data are not good enough to reveal the true complexity.

#### Continuum Models

Models for the smooth broad-band X-ray spectral shape of ULXs, ignoring narrow emission/absorption lines for the moment. 

The first model was borrowed from GBHBs, which contains disk thermal component and power-law component, and that looked very exciting for IMBHs.

But then the problem appears. In real GBHB thermal states, the disk dominates the X-ray spectrum, especially in the 2–10 keV band. In these ULX fits, however, the power-law component dominates above about 1 keV. That is already suspicious: the spectrum is not really behaving like a clean sub-Eddington thermal disk state.

<img src="./Notes%20from%20Literatures.assets/image-20260704152304420.png" alt="image-20260704152304420" style="zoom:50%;" />

The stronger result is that the hard component is not actually a simple power law. In high-quality XMM-Newton and NuSTAR spectra it curves or turns over at only a few keV, far below the $\sim 60\ \rm keV$ breaks usually seen in Galactic black-hole hard states.

So the "cool disk + power law" fit should not be read literally as an IMBH disk plus standard corona. A more physical picture is that the spectrum belongs to an **ultraluminous state**, probably connected with super-Eddington accretion.

>  #### UL Spectral Regimes
>
>  **Hard ultraluminous (hard UL)**: the harder component dominates, possibly because we are looking closer to the central funnel.
>
>  **Soft ultraluminous (soft UL)**: the soft component dominates, probably because the wind/photosphere blocks more of the inner disk.
>
>  **Broadened disk (BD)**: the spectrum looks disk-like but too broad for a standard thin disk. It may indicate near-Eddington or mildly super-Eddington disks.

#### Supersoft ULXs

Supersoft ULXs emit most of their observed flux below $1-2\ \rm keV$. Their spectra are dominated by a cool blackbody-like component with $kT \sim 0.1\ \rm keV$, plus a weak harder tail.

These sources are important because they may be ordinary ULXs seen at high inclination. In that view, the inner hot disk is hidden by the wind, and we mainly see the cooler photosphere.

#### Absorption

Every ULX spectrum is affected by neutral absorption from the Milky Way, the host galaxy, and possibly material near the source. The problem is that the inferred absorption column depends strongly on the assumed continuum model.

Typical extra columns are around $\sim (1-3)\times 10^{21}\ {\rm cm^{-2}}$, but some sources have higher or variable absorption. Variable absorption is especially interesting because it suggests material close to the ULX, not only foreground gas in the host galaxy.

#### Spectral Variability

ULX spectra change with time, but the trends are not as simple as in standard Galactic black-hole states. Some sources become softer as they brighten, while others become harder.

The cool component often does not follow $L_{\rm disk}\propto T_{\rm in}^4$. In several ULXs the fitted soft component becomes cooler as the luminosity increases, closer to what we expect if the soft component comes from an expanding wind/photosphere rather than a stable thin disk.

Some ULXs also jump between spectral regimes. For example, a source can look hard UL in one observation and broadened-disk-like in another. This is evidence that both accretion rate and viewing angle matter.

#### X-ray Evidence for Outflows

Soft X-ray residuals are not just fitting annoyances. In several high signal-to-noise ULX spectra, they can be interpreted as emission and absorption from ionized gas moving at mildly relativistic speeds.

The key result is that some ULXs show blueshifted absorption consistent with winds at $v \sim 0.2c$. This is direct X-ray evidence that ULXs launch powerful outflows, a major expectation of super-Eddington accretion.

### X-ray Timing

X-ray timing can reveal the nature of the accretor, the binary orbit, and the accretion state. The difficulty is that ULXs are distant, so timing studies need many photons or long monitoring campaigns.

#### Ultraluminous X-ray Pulsars

The discovery of coherent pulsations in M82 X-2 was a turning point. A coherent pulse means the accretor is a rotating neutron star, not a black hole, yet the apparent luminosity is above $10^{40}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$.

The paper discusses three ULX pulsars known then: M82 X-2, NGC 7793 P13, and NGC 5907 ULX-1. NGC 5907 ULX-1 reaches about $10^{41}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$, roughly hundreds of times the Eddington luminosity of a neutron star.

This proves that at least some ULXs are not IMBHs. It also proves that super-Eddington luminosities by very large factors can occur in real X-ray binaries.

#### Incoherent Fast Timing

High-frequency QPOs are interesting because their frequency can scale roughly as inverse black-hole mass. M82 X-1 has a claimed pair of QPOs in a $3:2$ ratio, giving a possible mass of a few hundred $M_{\odot}$ if they are truly analogous to high-frequency QPOs in Galactic black-hole binaries.

Low-frequency QPOs and broadband noise are less clean. They can appear in ULXs such as M82 X-1, NGC 5408 X-1, NGC 1313 X-1, and NGC 6946 X-1, but using them for black-hole mass estimates is controversial because the state identification is uncertain.

Fast variability also correlates with spectral type. Hard UL spectra are often relatively quiet, while soft UL spectra can show strong variability. The variable part often matches the hard spectral component, supporting a picture where clumpy wind material partly obscures the inner disk.

>  #### QPO
>
>  A quasi-periodic oscillation is a peak in the variability power spectrum. It is not a perfectly stable clock, but it tells us that some characteristic radius or timescale in the accretion flow is repeatedly modulating the X-ray light.

#### Long-term Periodicities and Quasi-periodicities

Long periods can be orbital, superorbital, or produced by precession. If the period is orbital, it is extremely valuable because it constrains the companion star and can lead to dynamical mass measurements.

The examples are complicated: M82 shows a 62-day period, NGC 5408 X-1 showed a $\sim 115$ day modulation that later shifted or disappeared, and NGC 5907 ULX-1 showed a $\sim 78$ day periodicity. Such behavior may reflect disk or jet precession rather than clean orbital periods.

Eclipsing ULXs in M51 are especially useful because eclipses imply high inclination. If their orbital periods can be confirmed, they become strong candidates for geometric and dynamical constraints.

### Multi-Wavelength Conterparts

Optical, UV, IR, and radio data tell us things that X-rays alone cannot: the companion star, nebulae, jets, winds, and the local stellar environment.

#### Optical Photometry

Point-like optical counterparts are usually faint and blue. At first glance this might suggest OB-type companions, but multi-band photometry often does not fit a normal stellar spectrum.

The X-ray to optical flux ratios resemble active low-mass X-ray binaries more than high-mass X-ray binaries. Therefore the optical light is often dominated by reprocessed emission from the accretion flow, not by the donor star itself.

This is why direct companion-star identification is difficult: the disk and wind can outshine the star.

#### Nebular Counterparts

Many ULXs sit inside large optical or radio nebulae, with sizes of tens to hundreds of parsecs.

Photoionized nebulae are useful because lines such as He II $\lambda4686$ and [O IV] require high-energy photons. Their luminosities can estimate the long-term average ionizing luminosity of the ULX. For Holmberg II X-1, the nebular emission argues against strong narrow beaming, because a narrow beam would not illuminate such a broad region.

Shock-powered nebulae indicate mechanical outflows. Their total energies can be around $10^{52}\ \rm erg$, larger than a normal supernova, and the mechanical power can be comparable to or larger than the X-ray luminosity.

#### Compact Radio Counterparts

Compact radio cores or jets are important because of the black-hole fundamental plane, which relates radio luminosity, X-ray luminosity, and black-hole mass.

But the fundamental plane should only be used when the source is in a hard-state-like, compact-jet regime. Radio flares during state transitions, such as in HLX-1, do not cleanly obey that relation.

NGC 2276-3c was proposed as an IMBH candidate from a compact radio jet and high X-ray luminosity, but the detection was disputed. The paper treats it as interesting but not settled.

#### Optical Evidence for Outflows

Optical spectra of ULXs often show broad Balmer and He II lines with widths of hundreds to more than $1000\ {\rm km\ s^{-1}}$.

If these lines came from a normal irradiated disk, the higher-ionization He II line should usually be broader than H-alpha because it forms closer to the compact object. In several ULXs the opposite trend appears, supporting a wind origin.

M81 ULS-1 shows blueshifted H-alpha corresponding to $0.14c-0.17c$, evidence for a baryonic jet. The analogy with SS 433 is strong: a super-Eddington Galactic binary with jets, winds, and a large radio nebula.

#### Companion Stars and Mass Constraints

NGC 7793 P13 is special because the optical spectrum actually shows the companion star, a B9Ia supergiant. Modeling constrains the compact object mass to be below about $15M_{\odot}$, and X-ray pulsations identify it as a neutron star.

M101 ULX-1 may have a Wolf-Rayet companion. Its radial velocity data allow a range of compact-object masses, but the line may be contaminated by disk or wind emission, so the mass is not secure.

The general lesson is that optical dynamical masses are hard for ULXs because the accretion flow is usually bright and messy.

#### Stellar Environments

ULXs in star-forming galaxies are often near young stellar associations, with ages of roughly $10-20\ \rm Myr$, but not always inside bright H II regions. This suggests that ULXs may turn on after the earliest massive-star phase.

In starburst galaxies, bright ULXs can lie near dense young clusters, where dynamical formation channels or massive stellar progenitors are more plausible.

### Super-Eddington Accretion

#### Evidence for Super-Eddington Accretion

The strongest evidence is the ULX pulsars: neutron stars are seen at luminosities far above their Eddington limits. For black-hole ULXs, the case comes from high luminosities, non-standard spectral states, powerful winds, and mechanical nebulae.

Super-Eddington accretion is not only "high radiation." A large part of the released energy may leave as kinetic power in winds and jets.

#### Mass Transfer

To power a luminosity $L_{\rm Bol}$, the mass transfer rate must satisfy
$$
\dot{m} = \frac{L_{\rm Bol}}{\eta c^2}.
$$
For $\eta \sim 0.1$, $10^{39}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$ already needs $\dot{m}\sim 10^{-7}M_{\odot}\ {\rm yr^{-1}}$.

Ordinary wind-fed or transient Galactic systems usually cannot maintain such high rates. The favored channel is thermal-timescale mass transfer from a massive companion that fills its Roche lobe. This can naturally provide highly super-Eddington mass supply for a finite evolutionary phase.

#### Neutron-Star Accretion

For neutron-star ULXs, the magnetic field is central. The accretion disk is truncated at the magnetospheric radius, where magnetic pressure balances the inflow. If that radius moves too far out, the propeller effect can stop accretion and produce low states.

>  #### Propeller Effect
>
>  If the neutron-star magnetosphere rotates faster than the inner disk can orbit, incoming gas is flung away instead of accreted. The source can then drop into a faint state.

Strong magnetic fields may help a neutron star exceed the Eddington limit by channeling gas into columns and reducing scattering opacity for some photon polarizations. Mild beaming may also help, but the nearly sinusoidal pulse profiles argue against extremely narrow beams.

Whether most ULXs are neutron stars is still unclear. Neutron-star binaries should be more common than black-hole binaries, but known ULX pulsars show unusually large variability, so they may be a recognizable subset rather than the whole population.

#### Black Hole Accretion

For black holes, the key idea is that a disk is not spherical. Radiation escapes preferentially away from the disk surface, while gas moves inward through the disk. Therefore the classical Eddington argument is not a simple global stop sign.

At super-Eddington rates, the inner disk becomes thick and launches a massive radiation-pressure-driven wind. Inside the spherization radius, excess material is expelled, and the central emission can be geometrically collimated into a funnel.

>  #### Spherization Radius
>
>  The spherization radius is the radius inside which the disk locally reaches the Eddington limit and begins to lose mass through a strong wind. Outside it, the disk can look more like an ordinary thin disk.

The paper discusses several theoretical pictures: very thick "Polish doughnut" disks, slim disks with strong advection, and porous/leaky disks. Modern simulations broadly support the important ingredients: a thick inner flow, a strong outflow, and a low-density funnel.

This geometry explains the spectral regimes:

1.  Looking down the funnel: we see more hot inner disk emission, giving hard UL spectra.
2.  Looking through the wind: the spectrum becomes softer and more variable, giving soft UL spectra.
3.  Looking at high inclination: the inner disk is hidden and the cool photosphere dominates, giving SSUL spectra.

It also explains why soft UL spectra can be more variable. Clumpy wind material crosses our line of sight and modulates the hard inner-disk component.

The scenario is attractive but not complete. Some mHz QPOs, soft lags, and additional hard components above $10\ \rm keV$ still need better physical explanation.

### IMBH Candidates

The review treats IMBHs as black holes between ordinary stellar remnants and SMBHs, roughly $10^2-10^5M_{\odot}$. ULXs above the star-forming galaxy XLF break, especially HLXs with $L_X \gtrsim 10^{41}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$, are the most interesting IMBH candidates.

But contamination is severe. Background AGN and foreground stars can mimic HLXs, so optical identification and host-galaxy association are essential.

#### ESO 243-49 HLX-1

HLX-1 is the strongest IMBH candidate in this review. Its luminosity peaks above $10^{42}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$, it is off-nuclear, and it shows state transitions resembling Galactic black-hole binaries.

The disk component follows approximately $L_{\rm disk}\propto T_{\rm in}^4$, which supports a standard-disk interpretation. Depending on spin and disk modeling, mass estimates range from a few $10^3M_{\odot}$ up to $\sim 10^5M_{\odot}$.

The H-alpha line confirms association with ESO 243-49. The optical counterpart may be a massive star cluster, and its velocity offset suggests HLX-1 could be the stripped nucleus of a dwarf satellite galaxy.

#### M82 X-1

M82 X-1 reaches nearly $10^{41}\ {\rm erg\ s^{-1}}$. Its best IMBH evidence comes from a possible $3:2$ high-frequency QPO pair, which would imply a mass around a few hundred $M_{\odot}$ if the analogy with Galactic black-hole QPOs is correct.

Spectral/timing changes also support a mass of hundreds of solar masses in some models, though source confusion in M82 makes the measurements delicate.

M82 X-1 lies near the young dense cluster MGG 11, where runaway stellar collisions could plausibly form an IMBH.

#### Other Candidates

Some HLXs have hard power-law-like spectra and strong variability, resembling a sub-Eddington hard state. If true, their high luminosities would require high black-hole masses.

But the evidence is usually weaker than for HLX-1 or M82 X-1. The paper's stance is cautious: several candidates are interesting, but most require better localization, optical follow-up, and cleaner spectral/timing evidence.

### Implications

#### Super-Eddington Accretion in Active Galactic Nuclei

Super-Eddington accretion may be necessary for growing high-redshift quasars so early in the Universe. ULXs therefore provide nearby laboratories for accretion physics that may also apply to young SMBH seeds.

The difference is fuel supply. ULXs accrete from companion stars, while AGN accrete from the surrounding gas reservoir, so AGN inflow can be more strongly limited by radiative and mechanical feedback.

#### X-Ray Binaries in Early Galaxies

Low metallicity increases ULX production per unit star formation. This matters because early galaxies were metal-poor, so X-ray binaries may have been much more important for heating the intergalactic medium than present-day galaxies suggest.

ULX spectra also matter for reionization-era 21 cm science: harder X-rays travel farther before being absorbed, changing the spatial pattern of IGM heating.

ULX outflows may also help clear gas and dust from star-forming regions, allowing Lyman continuum radiation to escape.

#### Gravitational Wave Sources

The first LIGO black-hole merger involved black holes more massive than typical Milky Way stellar black holes. Such masses are easier to form in low-metallicity environments, the same environments that appear efficient at producing ULXs.

Therefore ULXs are useful for testing binary-evolution pathways that may also produce gravitational-wave sources.
